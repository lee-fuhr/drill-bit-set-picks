Drill Bit Set questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A quality drill bit set should match the materials you most often drill into, wood, metal, masonry, or all three. Look for sets with a wide size range, durable coatings like titanium or black oxide, and a sturdy storage case that keeps bits organized. For most homeowners, a 20- to 30-piece mixed set with a sensible mix of twist bits, spade bits, and masonry bits will cover nearly every job without breaking the bank.

What types of drill bits are included in a good set?

A well-rounded set typically includes twist bits (general-purpose drilling in wood, plastic, and metal), brad-point bits (for clean holes in wood), spade bits (for larger holes in wood), and masonry bits (for brick, block, or concrete). Some sets also add step bits for sheet metal or drywall bits. Avoid sets that throw in oddball bits you’ll never use. Instead, look for one that covers common sizes such as 1/16-inch up to 1/2-inch for twist bits, and a handful of spade bits around 3/4-inch to 1-inch. A quality set will label each bit clearly so you can grab the right one quickly.

How do I choose the right drill bit set for my needs?

Start by thinking about what you’ll drill most often. If you’re a general DIYer working on furniture assembly, hanging shelves, and occasional light metal drilling, a 20-piece mixed set with a titanium or black oxide coating will handle most tasks. For masonry work, make sure the set includes at least three or four masonry bits with carbide tips. If you’re a woodworker who needs precision, a set of brad-point or Forstner bits may serve you better than a general-purpose set. For heavy-duty metal drilling, look for bits with a 135-degree split point and a gold oxide coating, which reduces walking and stays sharp longer. Don’t overbuy: a smaller, curated set often outperforms a huge cheap set full of flimsy bits.

What do different drill bit coatings do?

Coatings aren’t just for looks. Black oxide provides mild corrosion resistance and helps bits slide through wood and plastic. Titanium nitride (gold) extends bit life and reduces friction, making it a great choice for metal drilling. Diamond-coating exists but is expensive and usually saved for glass or tile bits. Uncoated high-speed steel (HSS) bits are the most affordable but dull faster. For occasional use, coated bits are worth the small premium. Just know that coatings wear off over time, especially if you let bits overheat, so treat them gently and use the correct speed for the material.

How should I store and organize drill bits?

A decent drill bit set comes with a plastic or metal storage case that holds each bit in its own slot. This is non-negotiable: storing bits loose in a toolbox leads to lost, dented, or scratched bits. Some sets use a sturdy clamshell case; others offer a hard-case with a transparent cover that lets you see sizes at a glance. For custom storage, many DIYers switch to a magnetic bar or a small drawer organizer, but that works best if you already own a complete set and separate out frequently used bits. Regardless of method, keep bits clean and dry. A drop of light oil after heavy use in metal or wet wood helps prevent rust.

What sizes should I look for in a drill bit set?

For most homeowners, a set that covers 1/16-inch to 1/2-inch by 64ths or 16ths is ideal. That gives you enough tiny bits for pilot holes and enough mid-size bits for cabinet hardware and wall anchors. You may rarely use the very smallest or very largest bits in the set, but having them is handy. If you do a lot of electrical or plumbing work, consider a set that includes longer bits (say 6-inch or 12-inch lengths) for drilling through studs. For masonry or tile, you’ll want separate sets because the bits have different shank designs and carbide tips. Don’t rely on a general set for those materials.

Can drill bits be sharpened or replaced?

Yes, many drill bits can be sharpened using a bench grinder or a dedicated drill bit sharpener, especially if you own bits with thick steel and decent geometry. However, cheap bits often lose their temper quickly and aren’t worth the sharpening effort. It’s usually faster and more reliable to replace them. For a basic toolkit, replacing a worn or broken bit costs just a few dollars per bit. If you buy a set that sells refill packs (like common twist bits in multi-size packs), you can replenish the most-used sizes without buying a whole new set. High-end bits from brands like Norseman or DeWalt can be sharpened multiple times before they’re worn out.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best drill bit set for general household use?

A 20- to 30-piece set containing black oxide or titanium twist bits from 1/16 inch to 3/8 inch, plus a few spade bits and masonry bits, covers most tasks. Reputable brands like Irwin, DeWalt, and Milwaukee offer such sets at a reasonable price. Avoid overly large sets made of unknown steel.

Are titanium drill bits better than black oxide?

Titanium nitride bits stay sharper longer and require less pressure in metal drilling, making them a better choice for frequent use on hard materials. Black oxide bits are more affordable and work fine for wood and occasional metal. Both are far better than plain HSS bits.

How long do drill bits last?

Lifespan depends on material, speed, and pressure. A quality twist bit used properly in wood can last hundreds of holes. Drilling a few dozen holes in steel will dull it noticeably. With gentle use and proper cooling, a set can last several years before you need to replace a few sizes.

Can I use wood bits on metal?

Not recommended. Wood bits have a pointed tip designed to center in wood, but they lack the hardened edge needed for metal. Attempting to drill metal with a wood bit will either dull it quickly or cause the bit to wander. Always use HSS or cobalt twist bits for metal.

What is the difference between a twist bit and a spade bit?

A twist bit is a cylindrical bit with helical flutes that drills small to medium holes (up to about 1/2 inch) in many materials. A spade bit is flat with a wide paddle shape, used for drilling larger holes (1/4 inch to 1 1/2 inches or more) in wood only. Spade bits are faster but leave rougher edges.

How do I know if a drill bit is sharp enough?

A sharp bit cuts smoothly and produces fine chips or dust. A dull bit will require more pressure, generate heat and friction, and may produce ragged holes or smoke. If you find yourself pushing hard on the drill to make progress, it’s time to sharpen or replace the bit.

In shortBuying a drill bit set isn’t about collecting the most bits, it’s about getting the right mix of sizes, coatings, and bit types for the work you actually do. Stick with a modest, well-made set from a trusted tool brand, store it properly, and respect the limits of each bit type. That approach will save you frustration, money, and ruined materials for years to come.
